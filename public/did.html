Dissociative identity disorder

Dissociative identity disorder (DID), also known as multiple personality disorder or split personality disorder, is a mental health illness in which you have two or more distinct identities.

"Dissociate" is to separate or disconnect. People with dissociative identity disorder may experience many personas, known as alters and every identity may exhibit distinct expressions, memories, mental patterns, and behaviors. The identities may differ in terms of gender, ethnicity, and how they interact with their surroundings.

Your behavior may be influenced by these personalities at different times, but memories may not transfer between identities, resulting in amnesia (memory gaps).

It is typically a result of severe and prolonged childhood trauma. Unable to cope with the stress, the mind makes multiple alternate personalities as a defense mechanism. These personalities can take over a personâ€™s consciousness at random times, often without their awareness.

Possession: Identities appear as though your body were taken over by an external entity or spirit. Others may notice a change in the way you speak or behave. The personality change is involuntary, and the identity is undesirable.

Nonpossession: People don't know as much about you. Instead of being in charge of your words, feelings, or actions, you may experience a rapid shift in how you identify yourself, as though you are watching yourself in a movie (an "out-of-body" experience).
