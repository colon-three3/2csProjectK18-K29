Schizophrenia

Written by dr who

Schizophrenia involves the disintegration of the relationship between thought, emotion, and behavior. It's a chronic mental disorder that a person's actions can even imply that they are out of touch with reality. Schizophrenia can be highly disturbing to both the person afflicted and those around them. Although researchers have yet to identify a definitive cause of schizophrenia, it is believed to be multifactorial, resulting from genetic malfunctions, brain chemistry complications, and environmental factors.

Symptoms associated with schizophrenia are cognitive in nature. The presence of hallucinations and hearing voices are some of the of the more common symptoms associated with those diagnosed with schizophrenia. Negative symptoms reflect a decrease in ability. For example, lack of emotional responsiveness, lack of motivation, or social apathy represent negative symptoms. Finally, cognitive symptoms are associated with challenges in attention, memory, and difficulty making decisions.

Schizophrenia manifests mostly in late adolescence or early adulthood, with approximately one percent of the population diagnosed worldwide. Symptoms are diagnosed through observation, and other comorbidities are ruled out through social screening and medical history. There is no direct blood test for diagnosis.

Schizophrenia is not curable but is controllable, and antipsychotics are the primary treatment course, with medications that stabilize symptoms and decrease severity. Other forms of control come from therapeutic measures, rehabilitation options, and social services.
